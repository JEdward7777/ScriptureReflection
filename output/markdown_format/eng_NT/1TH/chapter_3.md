| Reference | Verse | Translation Notes |
|:---------:|-------|-------------------|
|1TH 3:1|Therefore, when we could bear it no longer, we chose to be left alone in Athens.|This verse conveys the urgency and deep concern of Paul and his companions for the Thessalonian church, reflecting their decision to leave them alone in Athens due to circumstances beyond their control. The phrase 'we could bear it no longer' expresses their longing to remain in connection with the Thessalonians, emphasizing their care and support for them despite physical separation.|
|1TH 3:2|and we sent Timothy, our brother and God’s servant in the gospel of Christ, to strengthen and encourage you in your faith.|In this verse, Paul emphasizes the role of Timothy as a supportive figure, a brother in faith, and a servant of God. The choice of words is meant to highlight Timothy’s purpose: to strengthen and encourage the believers in their faith, which aligns with the overall theme of pastoral care and community support throughout Paul's letters.|
|1TH 3:3|so that no one would be shaken by these afflictions. For you yourselves know that we are destined for this.|This verse speaks of the reality of trials and afflictions faced by believers. The encouragement provided by Paul highlights the expectation of hardship in the Christian faith, while reminding the recipients of their calling to endure such challenges.|
|1TH 3:4|For indeed, when we were with you, we kept telling you that we would face trials, just as it has happened, and you know.|This verse emphasizes the inevitability of suffering for believers, as previously indicated by Paul during his time with the Thessalonians. The term 'thlibo' (to suffer) indicates not just general suffering but can imply a pressing or crushing pressure, which Paul assures them was expected.|
|1TH 3:5|For this reason, when I could no longer bear it, I sent to know about your faith, fearing that the tempter might have tempted you and that our labor would be in vain.|In this translation, I focused on capturing the urgency and concern expressed by Paul regarding the faith of the Thessalonians. The original Greek suggests a fear that their faith may have been tested beyond their ability to endure, leading to a sense of futility in their efforts. The choice of words reflects the emotional weight of this concern and aligns with the overall themes of perseverance found throughout Paul's letters.|
|1TH 3:6|But now that Timothy has come to us from you and has brought us good news of your faith and love, and that you always have a good remembrance of us, longing to see us, just as we also long to see you.|This verse reflects the joy and encouragement brought by Timothy's report of the Thessalonians' faith and love, highlighting their positive remembrance of Paul and his companions. The use of the terms 'faith' and 'love' connects with themes present in many Pauline epistles, emphasizing the essential nature of these virtues in the Christian community.|
|1TH 3:7|Therefore, brothers, in all our distress and affliction, we were comforted about you through your faith.|The original text emphasizes the encouragement the apostles felt due to the faith of the Thessalonians amid their own hardships. The use of 'affliction' and 'necessity' reflects the intense struggles faced, while 'faith' indicates the source of their comfort and strength.|
|1TH 3:8|For now we live, if you stand firm in the Lord.|The Greek phrase 'νῦν ζῶμεν' literally translates to 'now we live,' signifying the present state of vitality and hope that comes from faithfulness. The word 'στήκητε' is translated as 'you stand firm,' which reflects perseverance in faith. This verse encapsulates the joy and spiritual vitality experienced by Paul and his companions due to the steadfast faith of the Thessalonians, emphasizing the reciprocal nature of faith and community.|
|1TH 3:9|How can we thank God enough for you, in return for all the joy we feel before our God because of you?|In this verse, the Apostle Paul expresses profound gratitude to God for the Thessalonian believers. The term 'thanksgiving' denotes a deep appreciation, while 'joy' emphasizes the happiness that their faith brings him. This translation aims to capture the heartfelt nature of Paul's sentiment, highlighting the relational aspect of gratitude and the joy found in their faithfulness before God.|
|1TH 3:10|As we pray night and day earnestly to see your face and supply what is lacking in your faith.|In this verse, Paul expresses his deep desire to see the Thessalonian believers and to help strengthen their faith. The Greek term "καταρτίσαι" (to equip or restore) highlights the pastoral role of teaching and nurturing believers in their faith journey. The emphasis on night and day reflects Paul's continual prayers for the Thessalonians' growth in faith.|
|1TH 3:11|Now may our God and Father himself, and our Lord Jesus Christ, direct our way to you.|In this verse, the apostle Paul expresses a desire for divine guidance in strengthening the relationship between himself and the Thessalonians. The phrase 'to direct our way' implies a request for God's guidance and support, emphasizing the need for both divine and mutual connection among believers.|
|1TH 3:12|And may the Lord cause you to increase and overflow in love for one another and for all people, just as we do for you.|In this verse, Paul expresses a heartfelt desire for the believers' love to increase abundantly. The use of 'πλεονάσαι' (to abound) and 'περισσεύσαι' (to overflow) indicates a strong wish for love to grow significantly, both towards fellow believers and to everyone. The translation captures the essence of this longing for a rich and overflowing love within the Christian community.|
|1TH 3:13|so that He may strengthen your hearts, making them blameless in holiness before our God and Father at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ with all His saints.|This translation aims to maintain the emphasis on the importance of being established and blameless in holiness before God, particularly in light of the Lord's coming. The words 'strengthen,' 'hearts,' 'blameless,' and 'holiness' are highlighted to resonate with similar themes found in the surrounding texts.|
