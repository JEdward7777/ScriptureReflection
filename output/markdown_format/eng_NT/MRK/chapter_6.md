| Reference | Verse | Translation Notes |
|:---------:|-------|-------------------|
|MRK 6:1|Then He went out from there and came to His own hometown, and His disciples followed Him.|In this verse, the term 'patrida' translates as 'hometown', which conveys a sense of familiarity and belonging. The phrase 'akolouthousin auto' means 'follow Him', indicating the disciples' commitment and loyalty. The translation captures the essence of Jesus returning to where He was known and the presence of His disciples who support Him.|
|MRK 6:2|And when the Sabbath came, He began to teach in the synagogue; and many who heard Him were astonished, saying, "Where did this man get these things? And what is this wisdom that has been given to Him, and how are such miracles performed by His hands?"|In this verse, the astonishment of the people is expressed through questions regarding the source of Jesus' wisdom and the miraculous works performed by Him. The words used imply a sense of wonder and disbelief at His abilities, especially in contrast to His humble origins.|
|MRK 6:3|Is this not the carpenter, the son of Mary, and the brother of James and Joses and Judas and Simon? And are not His sisters here with us?" And they took offense at Him.|This verse emphasizes the familiarity of Jesus within His own community, highlighting the disbelief that arose among those who knew Him well. The term 'carpenter' ('τέκτων') reflects His earthly profession, while references to His familial relationships underscore the ordinary background from which He came, juxtaposed with the extraordinary claims of His ministry.|
|MRK 6:4|But Jesus said to them, "A prophet is without honor only in his own country, among his relatives, and in his own household."|This verse highlights the common theme of rejection faced by prophets, particularly in their own communities and families. The wording is tailored to capture the essence of Jesus’ remark about familiarity breeding contempt, emphasizing His unique status as a prophet. The translation aims for clarity while remaining faithful to the original Greek structure.|
|MRK 6:5|And He was unable to perform any mighty work there, except for laying His hands on a few sick people and healing them.|The original Greek text emphasizes the limitations faced by Jesus in His hometown due to the lack of faith among the people. This verse highlights the contrast between His divine ability and the reception He received from those familiar with Him. The use of 'power' and 'heal' reflects His authority and the effectiveness of His ministry, limited not by His power, but by the people's faith.|
|MRK 6:6|And He marveled at their unbelief. Then He went around the villages, teaching in their synagogues.|The phrase 'and He was amazed at their unbelief' reflects a profound sense of surprise and disappointment in response to a lack of faith. The verb 'περιῆγε' (He went about) indicates a continuous action of teaching, highlighting the mission and commitment of Jesus despite the opposition he faced in his hometown.|
|MRK 6:7|And He summoned the twelve and began to send them out two by two, giving them authority over unclean spirits.|The translation captures the sense of Jesus empowering His disciples for ministry by sending them out in pairs, emphasizing their authority over unclean spirits. The phrase 'two by two' signifies teamwork and mutual support in their mission.|
|MRK 6:8|And He instructed them to take nothing for the journey except a staff—no bread, no bag, and not even a copper coin in their belts.|In this verse, Jesus instructs His disciples to travel light and rely on God's provision. The terms 'staff,' 'bag,' and 'bread' are contrasted with the simple directive to take only a rod, emphasizing trust in God rather than material possessions. The mention of 'copper' refers to money or means to purchase necessities, which is also discouraged to highlight dependence on divine support.|
|MRK 6:9|But wear sandals, and do not put on two tunics.|This verse highlights the instruction given to the disciples regarding their attire and preparation for the journey. The focus is on being minimally equipped and ready for action, symbolizing reliance on God's provision rather than personal possessions.|
|MRK 6:10|And He said to them, 'Wherever you enter a house, stay there until you depart from that place.'|The phrase 'wherever you enter a house' indicates a focus on hospitality and the importance of remaining in one place during the ministry to establish relationships and credibility. The verb 'stay' implies not just physical presence but also a period of relational engagement. This directive also carries a cultural significance in that homes are places of safety and community.|
|MRK 6:11|And wherever they do not receive you or listen to you, as you leave there, shake off the dust that is under your feet as a testimony against them. Truly, I say to you, it will be more bearable for Sodom and Gomorrah on the day of judgment than for that city.|In this verse, the instruction to shake the dust off one's feet signifies a formal act of rejection of those who refuse to listen. It emphasizes the seriousness of the message being delivered and serves as a testimony against those who reject it. The reference to Sodom and Gomorrah serves to highlight the dire consequences of rejecting the message of God, particularly in the context of judgment.|
|MRK 6:12|So they went out and preached that people should repent.|The phrase 'ἐκήρυσσον ἵνα μετανοήσωσι' emphasizes the act of preaching or proclaiming with the purpose of leading others to repentance. The verb form indicates a continuous action, suitable for the context of disciples actively spreading the message of repentance.|
|MRK 6:13|And they drove out many demons, and anointed numerous sick people with oil and healed them.|The translation aims to reflect the authority and compassion demonstrated by the apostles as they carried out Jesus' commands, particularly focusing on the acts of exorcism and healing which are prevalent themes in the Gospel narratives.|
|MRK 6:14|And King Herod heard of Him, for His name had become widely known; and he said, "John the Baptist has risen from the dead, and therefore these powers are at work in him."|In this verse, the recognition of Jesus by Herod highlights the rising awareness of Jesus' ministry and the confusion surrounding His identity, particularly in relation to John the Baptist. The phrase 'raised from the dead' indicates a belief in resurrection, linking John’s ministry to the miraculous events occurring through Jesus. The term 'dynamis' (translated as 'powers') reflects the miraculous authority attributed to Jesus' works.|
|MRK 6:15|Some said, 'It is Elijah,' while others declared, 'It is a prophet, like one of the prophets of old.'|This verse presents various opinions about Jesus’ identity, drawing connections to the prophets, specifically Elijah, which is significant in Jewish eschatology. The translation aims to capture the nuances of public perception regarding Jesus' prophetic role while retaining fidelity to the original Greek text.|
|MRK 6:16|But when Herod heard of it, he said, 'John, whom I beheaded, has been raised from the dead.'|In this verse, Herod expresses his belief that Jesus is John the Baptist risen from the dead, emphasizing the miraculous nature attributed to John and the confusion surrounding Jesus' identity. The wording aims to preserve the original meaning while making it clear to contemporary readers.|
|MRK 6:17|For Herod himself had sent and arrested John, and he bound him in prison because of Herodias, his brother Philip's wife, for he had married her.|This translation reflects the historical context of Herod's actions against John the Baptist, emphasizing the influence of Herodias in the situation. The word choices are consistent with traditional language while aiming for clarity.|
|MRK 6:18|For John was saying to Herod, "It is not lawful for you to have your brother's wife."|In this verse, John the Baptist directly addresses King Herod regarding the legality of his marriage to Herodias, highlighting the seriousness of moral law and its application to the ruler. The phrase 'not lawful for you' emphasizes the authoritative nature of John's message, asserting a clear biblical principle regarding marriage.|
|MRK 6:19|But Herodias harbored a grudge against him and wanted to kill him, but she could not.|This verse reflects the tension between Herodias' desires and the legal and moral constraints of the time, emphasizing her determination against John's influence. The phrase 'he could not' implies a limitation of Herod's power, influenced by external circumstances.|
|MRK 6:20|For Herod feared John, knowing him to be a righteous and holy man; and he kept him safe. When he heard him, he was greatly perplexed, yet he enjoyed listening to him.|In this verse, the terms used to describe John the Baptist emphasize his righteousness and holiness, which caused Herod to have a fearful respect for him. The phrasing also indicates that Herod not only imprisoned John but also listened to him, showing a conflict between his fear of John and the influence John had over him.|
|MRK 6:21|And when a suitable day arrived, Herod gave a banquet for his nobles, military commanders, and the leading men of Galilee.|This verse captures a significant moment in the narrative where Herod, amidst his festivities, is making a public display that ultimately leads to tragic consequences. The use of 'banquet' denotes not just a meal, but a formal gathering of influential individuals, indicating Herod's political and social stature.|
|MRK 6:22|And when Herodias’ daughter came in and danced, she pleased Herod and those reclining at the table with him. The king said to the girl, "Ask me for whatever you wish, and I will give it to you."|This translation emphasizes the cultural context of feasting and performance in Herod's court, portraying the daughter's dance as a source of pleasure that prompted Herod's response. The phrase 'ask of me' is translated to convey Herod's offer clearly and invitingly.|
|MRK 6:23|And he swore to her, "Whatever you ask of me, I will give you, up to half of my kingdom."|In this translation, emphasis is placed on the promise made by Herod, reflecting both the gravity of his vow and the significant nature of the request. The phrase 'half of my kingdom' is rendered in a way that conveys the grandeur of the offer, typical of royal speech.|
|MRK 6:24|And she went out and said to her mother, 'What shall I ask for?' And she said, 'The head of John the Baptist.'|The phrase 'what shall I ask' conveys the idea of a moment of decision or choice influenced by external factors, reflecting Herodias’ daughter's predicament as she consults her mother about the request. This captures the interplay of influence and desire present in the original Greek text.|
|MRK 6:25|And she went in immediately with haste to the king and requested, saying, 'I want you to give me right now on a platter the head of John the Baptist.'|In this verse, the phrase 'immediately with haste' signifies urgency and determination. The request made to the king reflects a boldness and a desire for a significant action. The term 'platter' is rendered here to imply a formal presentation of the head, which was a symbol of victory and power.|
|MRK 6:26|And the king, feeling distressed because of the oaths and those who were dining with him, did not wish to break his word to her.|The term 'regretful' reflects the king's emotional state upon realizing the implications of his promise, while 'because of the oaths and those who were reclining with him' indicates the social pressure that influenced his decision. The use of 'revoke' instead of 'negate' maintains the meaning without overly complicating the language.|
|MRK 6:27|And immediately, the king sent an executioner and commanded that his head be brought.|This verse captures the immediate action taken by King Herod after being pressured by Herodias's daughter. The term 'executioner' is rendered as 'sent one,' reflecting the Greek usage while maintaining clarity in English. The verb 'commanded' implies a forceful decree, aligning with the authority of the king.|
|MRK 6:28|So he went and beheaded him in the prison; and he brought the head on a platter and gave it to the girl, and the girl presented it to her mother.|The phrase 'head on a platter' is used metaphorically to represent a dramatic and ruthless act of execution. The emphasis is on the act of delivering the severed head, which signifies total defeat and humiliation of the victim.|
|MRK 6:29|When his disciples heard of it, they came and took his body and laid it in a tomb.|In this verse, the disciples' actions of taking the body and placing it in a tomb reflect the common practices of burial in Jewish customs. The word 'body' is represented by 'πτῶμα' (ptōma), which means a corpse, and 'tomb' is represented by 'μνημείῳ' (mnēmeiō), indicating a place of remembrance.|
|MRK 6:30|And the apostles gathered together unto Jesus, and reported unto him all things, both what they had done and what they had taught.|This translation captures the moment when the apostles return to Jesus and share all their experiences with Him. The Greek word 'ἀπήγγειλαν' (reported) is crucial in conveying the idea of delivering a message or account of their activities.|
|MRK 6:31|And He said to them, "Come away by yourselves to a desolate place and rest a little while." For many were coming and going, and they had no opportunity even to eat.|In this translation, the focus is on Jesus inviting the disciples to rest in solitude. The terms for 'desolate place' and 'rest' are retained to reflect the original intent of seeking respite from the crowds.|
|MRK 6:32|So they departed to a deserted place by boat, privately.|The Greek term 'ἐρῆμος' used here translates to 'desolate' or 'wilderness', which emphasizes solitude and the absence of populated areas. The context highlights a retreat to a place away from the crowds, reinforcing the need for rest and communion with God.|
|MRK 6:33|But many saw them departing and recognized them; and they hurried on foot from all the towns and arrived at the place ahead of them.|This verse captures the urgency and eagerness of the crowds as they pursue Jesus, highlighting their recognition of Him and the spontaneous nature of their gathering. The word 'many' emphasizes the large number of people who came from various cities, signifying Jesus' growing popularity and the deep desire of the people to be near Him.|
|MRK 6:34|And when Jesus came out, He saw a great multitude and was moved with compassion for them, because they were like sheep without a shepherd; and He began to teach them many things.|In this verse, the compassion of Jesus is highlighted as He observes the crowd. The metaphor of sheep without a shepherd conveys the idea of helplessness and the need for guidance, which is a common theme in the Scriptures.|
|MRK 6:35|As the day was getting late, His disciples came to Him and said, "This place is desolate, and the time is already advanced."|In this verse, the disciples observe the surrounding conditions—specifically the desolation of the area and the lateness of the hour—indicating the need for action. The phrase 'the hour is now very late' emphasizes urgency in the context of their concern for the multitude, similar to how they have expressed it in other accounts.|
|MRK 6:36|Send them away, so that they may go into the surrounding countryside and villages to buy food for themselves; for they do not have enough to eat.|In this verse, Jesus' disciples express a practical concern about the need for food in a remote area, which reflects their understanding of the situation. The use of the term 'send them away' indicates a desire to address the immediate problem of hunger, while the mention of 'surrounding villages and farms' emphasizes the rural context of their predicament. The Greek word 'ἀγοράσωσιν' implies purchasing food, which denotes the necessity of provision for themselves.|
|MRK 6:37|But he answered them, "You give them something to eat." And they said to him, "Should we go and spend two hundred denarii on bread to give them to eat?"|This verse illustrates the tension between the disciples' practical concerns and Jesus' call to take initiative in providing for others. The phrase 'give them something to eat' reflects the compassion expected from those who follow Him, while also hinting at the miraculous provision that Jesus is capable of.|
|MRK 6:38|But He said to them, "How many loaves do you have? Go and see." And when they had found out, they said, "Five loaves and two fish."|In this translation, I aimed to capture the straightforwardness of Jesus' inquiry about the disciples' provisions. The phrasing is simplified for clarity while maintaining the original intent of the text. The term 'loaves' and 'fish' are retained to highlight the specific food items mentioned in the context of the miraculous feeding.|
|MRK 6:39|And He instructed them to make everyone recline in groups upon the green grass.|The word 'ἀνακλῖναι' (to recline) conveys a sense of relaxation and gathering for a meal, which is significant in the context of hospitality and fellowship. The phrase 'πάντας συμπόσια' suggests organizing the people into groups or gatherings, reflecting the cultural practice of communal meals.|
|MRK 6:40|So they reclined in groups, one hundred and one hundred, and fifty and fifty.|The phrase 'in groups' emphasizes the organization and order in which people were seated. The repetition of 'groups' in the original text highlights the methodical approach taken in distributing food. The numbers 'one hundred' and 'fifty' reflect an arrangement that fosters clarity and structure.|
|MRK 6:41|And taking the five loaves and the two fish, he looked up to heaven, blessed them, and broke the loaves. He then gave them to his disciples to distribute to the people, and he divided the two fish among them all.|This verse depicts the act of Jesus performing a miracle through prayer and distribution, highlighting themes of gratitude and provision. The phrase 'looked up to heaven' reflects Jesus’ dependence on God the Father, while 'gave thanks' indicates the significance of blessing in the act of sharing food.|
|MRK 6:42|And everyone ate and was filled.|This translation reflects the miracle of Jesus feeding the multitude, emphasizing both the act of eating and the fullness experienced by the people. The verb used here denotes not just the act of eating, but a sense of complete satisfaction that transcends mere physical nourishment.|
|MRK 6:43|And they picked up twelve baskets full of fragments, and from the fish.|This verse illustrates the abundance of provision following Jesus' miracle of feeding the multitude. The term 'fragments' denotes the leftover pieces of food, emphasizing God's sufficiency. The 'twelve baskets' represent completeness, symbolizing the twelve tribes of Israel, showing that not only were the needs of the people met, but there was an overflowing abundance remaining.|
|MRK 6:44|And those who ate the loaves were five thousand men.|The phrase 'five thousand men' reflects the Greek word 'πεντακισχίλιοι,' which specifically denotes a large, round number, emphasizing the magnitude of the crowd that Jesus fed. This translation maintains clarity and fidelity to the original context of the feeding of the five thousand.|
|MRK 6:45|And immediately, He compelled His disciples to get into the boat and go ahead of Him to the other side, toward Bethsaida, while He dismissed the crowd.|This verse emphasizes the authority of Jesus in directing his disciples, illustrating both his leadership and his intention to be alone after a busy ministry event. The words 'immediately' and 'compelled' indicate urgency and necessity in Jesus' actions.|
|MRK 6:46|And after bidding farewell to them, He went away to the mountain to pray.|The phrase 'ἀποταξάμενος' implies a deliberate departure or separation, suggesting the importance of His retreat to pray. The term 'προσεύξασθαι' specifically refers to the act of praying, emphasizing Jesus' focus on communion with the Father.|
|MRK 6:47|And when evening came, the boat was in the middle of the sea, and He was alone on the land.|In this translation, the focus is on the separation of Jesus from His disciples, emphasizing the vastness of the sea and the isolation of Jesus on land, which enhances the theme of His divinity and solitude during prayer.|
|MRK 6:48|And He saw them straining at the oars, for the wind was against them. About the fourth watch of the night, He came to them, walking on the sea, and He intended to pass by them.|This verse depicts a miraculous event where Jesus approaches His disciples who are struggling against the wind and waves during the fourth watch of the night. The translation emphasizes the distress of the disciples and the calm authority of Jesus as He walks on water, indicating His divine power over nature. The phrase 'He intended to pass by them' can be understood as a demonstration of His omnipresence and the desire to reveal Himself to them in their moment of distress.|
|MRK 6:49|But when they saw Him walking on the sea, they thought He was a ghost, and they cried out in fear.|This verse depicts the disciples' fear and misinterpretation of Jesus’ miraculous act as they perceived Him walking on the sea. The term 'ghost' used in the previous translations corresponds with the Greek 'φάντασμα,' which means something akin to a phantom or apparition. This highlights their astonishment and confusion in the face of the supernatural.|
|MRK 6:50|For they all saw Him and were troubled. And immediately He spoke with them and said, "Take courage; it is I; do not be afraid."|In this verse, the Greek word 'θαρσεῖτε' conveys a strong encouragement to be bold or take heart, reflecting Jesus’ reassurance to His disciples in a moment of fear. The phrase 'ἐγώ εἰμι' signifies His presence and authority, connecting to the divine name given in the Old Testament. The translation aims to capture both the immediacy of His message and the calm assurance He brings.|
|MRK 6:51|Then He went up into the boat with them, and the wind calmed down. They were utterly astonished within themselves.|This verse highlights the calming of the storm and the reaction of the disciples, indicating a deeper spiritual realization of Jesus' power. The phrase 'they were utterly astonished' reflects both their fear and wonder at His authority over nature. The translation aims to capture the intensity of their emotions in light of their previous experiences with Jesus.|
|MRK 6:52|For they did not understand the significance of the loaves; instead, their hearts were hardened.|The original Greek uses the term 'πεπωρωμένη', which means 'hardened' or 'insensitive', reflecting a spiritual condition where understanding is impaired. The translation aims to convey not just the hardness of the heart but the lack of perception regarding the miracles of Jesus, especially in the context of their experience with the loaves and fishes. This verse serves as a critical commentary on the disciples' inability to grasp the significance of Jesus' power and presence among them.|
|MRK 6:53|When they had crossed over, they arrived at the land of Gennesaret and anchored there.|The term 'ἐπὶ τὴν γῆν' is often used in the New Testament to denote arriving at a land or place, particularly in the context of Jesus' ministry, while 'προσωρμίσθησαν' can indicate an anchoring or settling, often used in relation to boats or ships coming to shore.|
|MRK 6:54|And when they had stepped out of the boat, they immediately recognized him.|The phrase 'εὐθέως ἐπιγνόντες' suggests immediate recognition or understanding. The term 'ἐξελθόντων' indicates their exit from the boat, which is a significant moment as they transition from the sea to land. The contextual emphasis is on the recognition of Jesus upon their arrival at land.|
|MRK 6:55|They ran throughout the whole region and began to carry about on mats those who were sick, wherever they heard he was.|This verse highlights the active response of the people in the region as they sought out Jesus for healing. The use of 'spread' indicates the urgency and determination of the crowd to bring those in need to Him. 'Carried on mats' refers to the common practice of transporting the sick or disabled, showcasing the compassion and desperation of the individuals bringing them to Jesus.|
|MRK 6:56|And wherever he entered, into villages, cities, or farms, they laid the sick in the marketplaces and implored him that they might touch even the fringe of his garment. And as many as touched it were made well.|In this translation, the emphasis is placed on the act of touching Jesus’ garment as a means of healing, consistent with the themes of faith and healing found throughout the Gospels. The phrase 'the hem of his garment' is used to directly correlate with similar passages, highlighting the significance of Jesus’ attire in the healing narrative.|
