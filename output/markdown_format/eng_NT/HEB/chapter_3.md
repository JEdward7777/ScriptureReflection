| Reference | Verse | Translation Notes |
|:---------:|-------|-------------------|
|HEB 3:1|Therefore, holy brothers, partners in a heavenly calling, consider Jesus, the Apostle and High Priest of our confession.|This verse emphasizes the communal aspect of faith, addressing believers as 'holy brothers' and reminding them of their shared heavenly calling. It identifies Jesus as the Apostle and High Priest of their confession, highlighting His roles in both the church and the believer's life.|
|HEB 3:2|He was faithful to the one who appointed him, just as Moses was faithful in all of God's house.|This verse emphasizes the faithfulness of Jesus as the one appointed by God, paralleling the faithfulness of Moses. The term 'faithful' underscores reliability and trustworthiness in carrying out God's mission. In the context of Hebrews, it highlights the importance of Jesus as the faithful high priest compared to Moses, who served faithfully as God's servant.|
|HEB 3:3|For Jesus has been deemed worthy of greater glory than Moses, just as the builder of a house has more honor than the house itself.|In this verse, the comparison is drawn between Christ and Moses, highlighting the greater glory and honor of Christ as the builder of God's house compared to Moses, who served within it. The terms of 'greater glory' and 'honor' indicate a clear hierarchy, establishing Christ's superior status in the context of God's plan.|
|HEB 3:4|For every house is built by someone, but the one who built everything is God.|This translation seeks to emphasize the relationship between the builder and the house, highlighting the divine nature of God as the ultimate builder and architect of all creation.|
|HEB 3:5|And Moses indeed was faithful in all his house as a servant, for a testimony of those things which were to be spoken later.|In this verse, Moses is described as faithful within the context of God's household, emphasizing his role as a servant. The word 'faithful' is a key term that highlights loyalty and reliability, while 'house' denotes God's family or community.|
|HEB 3:6|But Christ as a Son over His own house, whose house we are if we hold fast the boldness and the rejoicing of the hope firm to the end.|This verse emphasizes the role of Christ as a faithful son over His own house, which refers to the believers who are part of His household. The use of 'boldness' (παρρησίαν) and 'boasting' (καύχημα) speaks to the confidence believers should maintain in their faith, anchored in the hope of Christ.|
|HEB 3:7|Therefore, as the Holy Spirit says: 'Today, if you hear His voice,'|The verse emphasizes the urgency of heeding the voice of the Holy Spirit, which is particularly relevant to the context of the Israelites in the wilderness. The phrase 'if you hear His voice' serves as a direct call to responsiveness and faithfulness. It is also important to note that 'today' denotes immediacy, contrasting with hard-heartedness that leads to disobedience.|
|HEB 3:8|Do not harden your hearts as in the rebellion, on the day of testing in the wilderness.|This verse warns against the hardness of heart that leads to disbelief and rebellion against God, using historical references to Israel's disobedience in the wilderness as a cautionary reminder for believers today. The original Greek term for 'harden' (σκληρύνητε) suggests a progressive hardening, reflecting a willful resistance to God’s guidance.|
|HEB 3:9|where your fathers tested me and tried me, and saw my works for forty years.|This verse reflects the testing of God by the ancestors of the Israelites during their time in the wilderness, specifically emphasizing the disobedience and lack of faith that resulted from witnessing God's works yet still choosing to rebel against Him. The term 'test' is repeated to highlight the significance of their actions in the context of their relationship with God over the 40 years in the desert.|
|HEB 3:10|Therefore I was provoked by that generation, and said: 'They always go astray in their hearts, and they have not recognized my ways;'|In this verse, the phrase 'I was provoked by that generation' conveys God's disappointment with Israel's consistent disobedience and lack of faith. The expression 'they always go astray in their hearts' reflects a persistent tendency to deviate from God's path, which is a central theme throughout scripture. The term 'my ways' emphasizes the divine guidance that was not recognized or followed by the people.|
|HEB 3:11|So I swore in my anger, ‘They shall never enter into my rest.’|The phrase 'I swore in my wrath' is a strong declaration indicating God's serious intention and the finality of His decision. The word 'rest' refers to God's promise of peace and fulfillment that He offers to His people, which can be understood both spiritually and physically.|
|HEB 3:12|See to it, brothers, that none of you has a heart of unbelief that turns away from the living God.|This verse warns against the danger of a heart turned away from God, reflecting the importance of faithfulness and vigilance in the Christian walk. The phrase 'evil heart of unbelief' emphasizes the moral condition that can lead to spiritual estrangement.|
|HEB 3:13|But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called 'today,' so that none of you may be hardened by sin’s deceitfulness.|The verse emphasizes the importance of encouraging one another daily to avoid the deceitfulness of sin which leads to a hardened heart. The phrase 'as long as it is called today' suggests the urgency of this encouragement in the present moment, underlining the necessity for constant vigilance in our spiritual lives.|
|HEB 3:14|For we have become partakers of Christ, if we hold firmly to the beginning of our assurance until the end.|The phrase 'partakers of Christ' refers to those who share in the life and promises of Christ through faith. The 'beginning of our assurance' suggests a firm foundation of faith that is expected to be held onto until the end. The exhortation emphasizes the importance of perseverance in faith amid challenges.|
|HEB 3:15|As it is said: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as they did in the rebellion."|The phrase 'if you hear his voice' emphasizes the urgency of responding to God's call, which is a recurring theme throughout scripture. The command not to harden one's heart illustrates the need for receptiveness and humility in the face of divine instruction. This translation maintains a direct connection to the original Greek phrasing while conveying its meaning in contemporary English.|
|HEB 3:16|For who were they who heard and yet rebelled? Was it not all those who came out of Egypt through Moses?|This verse highlights the collective disobedience of the Israelites who heard God's commands through Moses yet chose to rebel. The focus is on the consequences of their actions in light of the warning to heed God's voice.|
|HEB 3:17|And with whom was He angry for forty years? Was it not with those who sinned, whose bodies fell in the wilderness?|In this verse, the focus is on the rebellion of the Israelites during their forty years in the wilderness and God's displeasure with them due to their unfaithfulness. The term 'forty years' underscores the significant period of trial and testing. The phrase 'fell in the wilderness' indicates the consequence of their disobedience, pointing to both physical death and spiritual failure.|
|HEB 3:18|And to whom did He swear that they would not enter His rest, except to those who were disobedient?|The translation emphasizes the seriousness of the oath made by God concerning entering His rest. The term 'unbelieving' is aligned with the concept of disobedience expressed in earlier verses. The context suggests a warning about the consequences of disbelief, tying back to the Israelite experience in the wilderness.|
|HEB 3:19|And we see that they were unable to enter because of their lack of faith.|The phrase 'they could not enter' underscores the consequence of disbelief. The term 'unbelief' (ἀπιστίαν) in this context points to a deeper issue of trust and obedience towards God's promises. The translation aims to convey both the literal meaning and the theological implications of the verse.|
