| Reference | Verse | Translation Notes |
|:---------:|-------|-------------------|
|2TH 3:1|Finally, brothers, pray for us, that the word of the Lord may spread rapidly and be honored, just as it was with you.|The phrase 'the word of the Lord' reflects a common biblical motif, emphasizing the power and reverence of God’s message. The request for prayer is an appeal for divine assistance in spreading this message.|
|2TH 3:2|and that we may be delivered from unreasonable and wicked men; for not all have faith.|The Greek word 'ἀτόπων' implies actions or behaviors that are inappropriate or irregular, while 'πονηρῶν' suggests wicked or malicious intent. This translation emphasizes the need for deliverance from individuals whose faith does not align with the teachings of Christ, acknowledging the reality of varied beliefs among people.|
|2TH 3:3|But the Lord is faithful; he will strengthen you and protect you from the evil one.|In this verse, 'faithful' reflects God's unwavering nature and His commitment to His followers. The verb 'to establish' indicates a firm support, while 'to guard' implies protection against malevolent influences. The term 'evil one' corresponds closely to the original Greek concept of moral evil personified.|
|2TH 3:4|We have confidence in the Lord concerning you, that you are doing and will continue to do what we command.|The phrase "we have confidence in the Lord" emphasizes trust and assurance in the Lord regarding the actions of the believers. The imperative nature of 'commanding' suggests a strong encouragement for obedience in faith and practice.|
|2TH 3:5|But may the Lord direct your hearts into the love of God and into the patience of Christ.|This verse emphasizes the role of the Lord in directing our hearts toward love and endurance, mirroring the themes found in Paul's other letters regarding love and perseverance in Christ.|
|2TH 3:6|Now we command you, brothers, in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, to withdraw from every brother who walks in a disorderly manner and not according to the tradition which you received from us.|In this verse, Paul commands the believers to withdraw from any brother who walks disorderly and not according to the teachings they received. The use of 'ἀτάκτως' (disorderly) emphasizes the need for adherence to the established order and traditions passed down from the apostles. The reference to the authority of Jesus Christ adds weight to the command.|
|2TH 3:7|For you yourselves know how you ought to imitate us, because we were not disorderly among you.|In this verse, the Apostle Paul encourages the believers to recognize and emulate his conduct and way of life. The term 'imitate' is significant in the Christian faith as it emphasizes the importance of living in accordance with the teachings and example of faithful leaders.|
|2TH 3:8|We did not eat anyone’s bread without paying for it, but with toil and hard work, we labored night and day, so as not to burden any of you.|In this translation, I emphasize the themes of hard work and self-sufficiency. The original Greek terms related to toil and labor are preserved, illustrating the effort made by Paul and his companions while avoiding being a burden to the church in Thessalonica.|
|2TH 3:9|It is not that we lack the authority, but to give you an example to follow, so that you may imitate us.|This verse emphasizes the importance of setting a good example for others to follow, reflecting the apostolic authority coupled with the humility of living as a model rather than just demanding obedience.|
|2TH 3:10|For even when we were with you, we gave you this command: If anyone is not willing to work, let him not eat.|In this verse, Paul emphasizes the importance of personal responsibility and the necessity of working. He provides a clear directive against idleness, echoing principles found throughout the Scripture regarding the value of diligent work.|
|2TH 3:11|For we hear that there are some among you who walk in a disorderly manner, not working at all, but are busybodies.|The term 'ἀτάκτως' conveys a sense of disorderliness, suggesting those who are walking out of step with the community's standards or expectations. 'περιεργαζομένους' implies an excessive engagement in non-productive work or meddling in the affairs of others, which contrasts sharply with the Biblical emphasis on diligent labor.|
|2TH 3:12|Now we command and urge such ones, through our Lord Jesus Christ, to work quietly and eat their own bread.|This verse encourages diligent work and self-sufficiency within the Christian community. It emphasizes the importance of working quietly and providing for oneself, reflecting the values of industriousness and responsibility.|
|2TH 3:13|But you, brothers, do not grow weary in doing good.|In this verse, the apostle Paul emphasizes the importance of perseverance in doing good. The phrase 'mē ekkakēsate' literally translates to 'do not lose heart' or 'do not grow weary.' The encouragement to continue doing good reflects a key theme in the Pauline epistles about maintaining faithfulness in good works.|
|2TH 3:14|And if anyone does not obey our instruction through this letter, take note of that person and do not associate with him, so that he may be ashamed.|This verse emphasizes the importance of adhering to apostolic teaching and maintaining moral integrity within the community of believers. The terms for disobedience and marking those who do not follow sound instruction reflect the serious nature of the issue at hand. The phrase ‘to be shamed’ indicates a purpose for separation, encouraging repentance through the realization of one's error.|
|2TH 3:15|Yet do not regard him as an enemy, but warn him as a brother.|This verse emphasizes the importance of approaching those who may have strayed from correct conduct not with hostility but with a spirit of brotherly correction and encouragement. It conveys the essence of maintaining community and accountability among believers while fostering an attitude of love and support.|
|2TH 3:16|Now may the Lord of peace Himself grant you His peace at all times and in every way. The Lord be with you all.|In this translation, the emphasis is on the divine source of peace, affirming that it is the Lord who gives peace in every circumstance. The phrase 'at all times in every way' captures the universality of God's peace, emphasizing its constancy and applicability in all situations. The concluding affirmation that 'the Lord be with all of you' is a common blessing in Pauline letters, reflecting a sense of community and support among believers.|
|2TH 3:17|The greeting is by my own hand, Paul, which is a sign in every letter; thus I write.|In this verse, Paul emphasizes the personal touch in his letters by signing it with his own hand, indicating authenticity and authority. The phrase 'my own hand' is translated to maintain the emphasis on personal involvement, which is significant in ancient letter-writing practices to confirm the message's genuineness.|
|2TH 3:18|The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you all. Amen.|This verse concludes Paul's second letter to the Thessalonians with a blessing, echoing a common theme in Paul's epistles where he invokes the grace of Christ as a source of comfort and strength for believers. The phrase 'with all of you' emphasizes community and fellowship in grace.|
