| Reference | Verse | Translation Notes |
|:---------:|-------|-------------------|
|TIT 2:1|But as for you, speak the things which are fitting for sound doctrine.|The phrase 'what is proper' indicates a call to teach in accordance with sound doctrine that promotes health in the faith, reflecting a standard of teaching that aligns with godly living.|
|TIT 2:2|Older men are to be temperate, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness.|In this translation, I have aimed to retain the nuances of the Greek terms while providing clarity in English. The use of descriptors such as 'temperate' and 'dignified' reflects the seriousness of character that is being called for, as well as the balanced nature of faith and virtue. The list emphasizes qualities expected in mature believers, drawing from the context of living out one's faith effectively.|
|TIT 2:3|Likewise, older women are to be reverent in behavior, not slanderers or slaves to much wine, but teachers of good things.|In this verse, the focus is on the qualities of older women in the church community. The terms used emphasize the importance of reverence, self-control, and the teaching role that they fulfill. The use of the word 'reverent' (ἱεροπρεπεῖς) captures a sense of dignity that aligns with their respected position, while the prohibition against being slanderous and excessive in drinking reinforces their role as examples for younger women and the community at large.|
|TIT 2:4|that they may teach the young women to be sensible, to love their husbands, and to love their children.|The phrase 'to teach the young women to love their husbands' emphasizes the importance of nurturing love and familial bonds among younger women. The word 'sensible' (σωφρονίζωσι) suggests a teaching that cultivates self-control and wisdom in their roles as wives and mothers, aiming at establishing a harmonious family environment.|
|TIT 2:5|to be discreet, pure, keepers at home, kind, and submissive to their own husbands, so that the word of God may not be blasphemed.|This translation emphasizes the qualities of purity, self-control, and submission, reflecting the cultural context of the time while maintaining the theological significance of obedience to God. The phrasing has been adapted for clarity and relevance to contemporary readers while staying true to the original message.|
|TIT 2:6|In the same way, encourage the young men to be self-controlled.|The original Greek phrase 'Τοὺς νεωτέρους' specifically addresses younger men, while 'σωφρονεῖν' emphasizes the quality of self-control and soundness of mind. In this translation, the encouragement to younger men is coupled with the expectation of a virtuous character, consistent with the preceding themes of conduct and godliness.|
|TIT 2:7|In all things, show yourself to be a model of good works; in your teaching, exhibit integrity, dignity, and incorruptibility.|In this verse, Paul instructs Titus to present himself as a model of good works, emphasizing qualities that reflect integrity and seriousness in teaching. The term 'good works' underscores the importance of actions that demonstrate the faith and character of a believer. The use of 'incorruptibility' and 'dignity' highlights a standard of moral uprightness and respect in both life and instruction.|
|TIT 2:8|and sound speech that cannot be condemned, so that those who oppose you may be ashamed, having nothing evil to say about us.|The phrase 'λόγον ὑγιῆ' (sound word) emphasizes the importance of wholesome teaching that aligns with Christian doctrine. 'ἀκατάγνωστον' (unimpeachable) denotes a quality of integrity that cannot be called into question. This translation seeks to capture the original's intent while ensuring clarity and relevance for contemporary readers.|
|TIT 2:9|Slaves are to be submissive to their own masters in everything, aiming to be pleasing and not argumentative.|The term 'slaves' (δουλους) refers to those in servitude and denotes the importance of respect and obedience to authority in a Christian context. The phrase 'in everything pleasing' captures the essence of being agreeable and compliant in actions. The verb 'submit' (ὑποτάσσεσθαι) highlights a voluntary positioning under authority, which aligns with the overall theme of respect in Christian teachings.|
|TIT 2:10|not pilfering, but showing all good faith, so that in everything they may adorn the doctrine of our Savior, God.|In this verse, Paul emphasizes the importance of integrity and good works among slaves, aligning their behavior with the teachings of Christ. The terms used indicate a call for sincerity and respect, reflecting a lifestyle that adorns the doctrine of God.|
|TIT 2:11|For the grace of God that brings salvation has appeared to all men.|The phrase 'the grace of God' in this verse emphasizes God's unmerited favor, which is foundational for the concept of salvation offered to all humanity. The term 'appeared' highlights the moment of revelation and the manifestation of God's saving grace through Jesus Christ, which aligns with other New Testament teachings regarding salvation and the universal offer of grace.|
|TIT 2:12|Instructing us to deny ungodliness and worldly desires, and to live soberly, righteously, and godly in the present age.|This verse emphasizes the transformative power of grace, encouraging believers to reject ungodliness and worldly desires. The use of the terms 'soberly,' 'righteously,' and 'godly' highlights the moral and ethical standards expected from believers in their current lives, as they await the ultimate fulfillment of their hope in Christ.|
|TIT 2:13|awaiting our blessed hope, the glorious appearing of our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ.|This verse speaks of a hopeful anticipation for the future, characterized by the glorious return of Jesus Christ. It emphasizes the dual aspects of hope and revelation, underscoring the glory of God and the significance of Jesus as our Savior. The term 'great God' conveys the majesty and authority of God, while 'Savior' highlights the redemptive role of Christ. Thus, the translation reflects the essence of the original text without adhering to existing translations closely.|
|TIT 2:14|Who gave Himself for us, to redeem us from all lawlessness and to purify for Himself a people who are His very own, eager to do good works.|The verse speaks of Christ's sacrificial act and His purpose in redeeming and purifying His people, calling them a 'zealous people for good works.' This reflects both the idea of redemption and the resultant transformation of believers into a devoted community.|
|TIT 2:15|Declare these things; exhort and rebuke with all authority. Let no one disregard you.|The original Greek emphasizes the importance of speaking, encouraging, and correcting with authority. The phrase 'with all authority' reflects the seriousness of the role of the teacher or elder, implying that their words should be respected and heeded by the community. The instruction against contempt ('let no one disregard you') underscores the need for diligence in ministry and the authority given to leaders in the church.|
